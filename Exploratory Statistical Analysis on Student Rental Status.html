1 Introduction

The increasing cost of living has placed significant financial pressure on university students, particularly those who choose to rent accommodation while studying. Housing expenses can influence many aspects of student life, including spending habits, academic performance, and overall wellbeing. Understanding these relationships is important for identifying the challenges faced by students living independently and for informing future support initiatives. Previous reporting has highlighted that “Students are suffering from stress and anxiety caused by the pressures of housing costs” (Peachy, 2018).

This project investigates whether rental status is associated with differences in university students’ academic performance, weekly food expenditure, and preferred teaching structure. The analysis uses survey data collected from students enrolled in the University of Sydney’s DATA2002/DATA2902 Semester 2 cohort during 2025. The variables examined include rental status (Yes/No), preference for semester or trimester teaching, weekly expenditure on food and beverages, and Weighted Average Mark (WAM). Data cleaning, visualisation, and statistical analyses were conducted using R to produce reproducible results.

As the survey was distributed voluntarily via email, the dataset represents a convenience sample rather than a random sample of the broader student population. Consequently, the findings should not be interpreted as representative of all university students. Voluntary participation introduces the possibility of selection bias, as students who regularly engage with university emails may have been more likely to respond. In addition, several variables rely on self-reported information, creating the potential for recall bias and response bias. For example, participants may have estimated their weekly food expenditure or reported inaccurate WAM values.

Improvements could be made to questions asked in the survey, particularly in numerically based questions such as height, as many entries either had incorrect units or units labelled differently that made it hard to collate the data. Some other questions such as the amount of alcohol consumed may be sensitive and people wouldn’t want to respond a certain way, this could be limited by ensuring participants that all answers will be confidential.

1.1 Data cleaning

Prior to analysis, the dataset underwent a structured data-cleaning process. Missing values were removed, categorical responses were standardised, and observations with implausible values (such as unrealistic WAM scores or weekly food expenditure) were excluded. While these procedures improve data quality, some limitations remain. Certain survey questions permitted inconsistent units or free-text responses, requiring additional cleaning, and sensitive questions may have influenced the accuracy of participant responses despite assurances of anonymity.

2.1.1 Hypothesis Testing (Fisher’s Exact Test)

Hypotheses

  • Null Hypothesis (H0): Rent status and term preference are independent

  • Alternative Hypothesis (H1): Rent status and term preference are not independent

  • Each Result is independent from each other

  • Categories are mutually exclusive

Since the p-value is greater than 0.05 we fail to reject the null hypothesis, and therefore there is no evidence to suggest an association between rental status and term preference.

Limitations:

The findings should be interpreted with caution due to several limitations of the study. In addition, the final sample consisted of 259 participants, which limits the precision of the analysis. A larger and more diverse sample collected from multiple universities would improve the reliability of the estimates and increase the extent to which the findings can be generalised to the broader university student population.

2.2.1 Hypothesis Testing (Wilcoxon rank-sum test)

Hypotheses

  • Null Hypothesis (H0): No difference in the mean of weekly spending between those that rent and those that do not

  • Alternative Hypothesis (H1): There is a difference in the weekly mean between the two groups.

Assumptions

    This is confirmed true as every student filled out their individual survey and was locked to one response per person

  • The distribution follow the same distribution

    Based on the two histograms below in figure 4, the distribution is left skewed and so this assumption is passed

Test Statistic:

Code 
df_c4$rank = rank(df_c4$weekly_food_spend)
ranks_no_rent = df_c4$rank[df_c4$pay_rent == "No"]
ranks_yes_rent = df_c4$rank[df_c4$pay_rent == "Yes"]
n2 = sum(df_c4$pay_rent == "Yes")
n1 = sum(df_c4$pay_rent == "No")
w = sum(ranks_no_rent)
E_w = (n1*(n1 + n2 + 1))/2
V_w = (n1*n2*(n1 + n2+1))/12
Z = (w-E_w)/sqrt(V_w)

Z - Statistic = -5.81

p-value

Code 
p_value = 2*pnorm(Z)

6.18*10^-9

Decision

Since the p-value is significantly less than 0.05, we reject the Null hypothesis, and therefore there is a statistical difference between the average weekly spending of students who rent compared to those who do not.

Limitations:

The analysis is subject to several limitations. First, the sample size was relatively small, reducing the precision of the results. Collecting data from a larger sample would improve the reliability and generalisability of the findings.

In addition, weekly food expenditure was self-reported, making the results susceptible to recall bias, as participants may not accurately remember their typical weekly spending. During the data-cleaning process, four observations with reported weekly food expenditure exceeding $1,000 were removed, as these values were considered implausible and likely represented data entry errors. While this improves data quality, it is possible that excluding these observations may have had a small influence on the results.

2.3.1 Hypothesis Testing (Monte Carlo Simulation)

Hypothesis

The distribution of WAM is the same for students that rent and don’t rent

The distribution of WAM is not the same for students for students that rent or don’t rent.

Assumptions:

There are no assumptions in the Monte Carlo Simulation test

Sampling

Code 
Difference = mean(df_c4$wam[df_c4$pay_rent == "Yes"]) - mean(df_c4$wam[df_c4$pay_rent == "No"])
ns = 10000
sample_diff = numeric(ns)
set.seed(540697582)
for(i in 1:ns){
  sample_wam = sample(df_c4$wam) 
  sample_diff[i] = mean(sample_wam[df_c4$pay_rent == "Yes"]) - mean(sample_wam[df_c4$pay_rent == "No"])
}

p-value

Code 
p_value3 = mean(abs(sample_diff) >= abs(Difference))

0.0269

Decision

Since the p-value is smaller than 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis, and therefore there is evidence that the distribution of WAM is different for those who rent and those who do not

Limitations:

The findings should be interpreted with several limitations in mind. As WAM was self-reported, the results may be affected by response bias if participants reported inaccurate marks, either intentionally or unintentionally. The relatively small sample size of 291 participants also limits the precision and generalisability of the findings, and a larger sample would provide more reliable estimates.

3 Conclusion

This analysis investigated whether rental status is associated with differences in university students’ academic performance, weekly food expenditure, and preferred semester length. The results indicated no statistically significant association between rental status and preference for semesters or trimesters. However, statistically significant differences were observed in both weekly food expenditure and Weighted Average Mark (WAM) between students who rent and those who do not.

While these findings suggest that rental status may be associated with certain aspects of student life, they should be interpreted with caution. The data were collected from a non-random convenience sample of University of Sydney students, limiting the generalisability of the results to the wider university population. In addition, the relatively small sample size and the potential for selection, recall, and response bias may have influenced the findings.

Overall, this project demonstrates how statistical methods can be used to investigate relationships within survey data while highlighting the importance of considering study design and data limitations when interpreting results.

### Hypothesis Testing (Monte Carlo Simulation)

**Hypothesis**

The distribution of WAM is the same for students that rent and don't rent

The distribution of WAM is not the same for students for students that rent or don't rent.

**Assumptions:**

There are no assumptions in the Monte Carlo Simulation test

**Sampling**

```{r}
Difference = mean(df_c4$wam[df_c4$pay_rent == "Yes"]) - mean(df_c4$wam[df_c4$pay_rent == "No"])
ns = 10000
sample_diff = numeric(ns)
set.seed(540697582)
for(i in 1:ns){
  sample_wam = sample(df_c4$wam) 
  sample_diff[i] = mean(sample_wam[df_c4$pay_rent == "Yes"]) - mean(sample_wam[df_c4$pay_rent == "No"])
}
```

**p-value**

```{r}
p_value3 = mean(abs(sample_diff) >= abs(Difference))
```

**Decision**

Since the p-value is smaller than 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis, and therefore there is evidence that the distribution of WAM is different for those who rent and those who do not

**Limitations:**

The findings should be interpreted with several limitations in mind. As WAM was self-reported, the results may be affected by response bias if participants reported inaccurate marks, either intentionally or unintentionally. The relatively small sample size of 291 participants also limits the precision and generalisability of the findings, and a larger sample would provide more reliable estimates.

# **Conclusion**

This analysis investigated whether rental status is associated with differences in university students' academic performance, weekly food expenditure, and preferred semester length. The results indicated no statistically significant association between rental status and preference for semesters or trimesters. However, statistically significant differences were observed in both weekly food expenditure and Weighted Average Mark (WAM) between students who rent and those who do not.

While these findings suggest that rental status may be associated with certain aspects of student life, they should be interpreted with caution. The data were collected from a non-random convenience sample of University of Sydney students, limiting the generalisability of the results to the wider university population. In addition, the relatively small sample size and the potential for selection, recall, and response bias may have influenced the findings.

Overall, this project demonstrates how statistical methods can be used to investigate relationships within survey data while highlighting the importance of considering study design and data limitations when interpreting results.
